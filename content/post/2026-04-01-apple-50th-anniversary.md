Apple was founded on April 1, 1976 — fifty years ago today.

I can't say I grew up with Apple devices: ours was an IBM PC and eventually a
Windows NT household because that's what Mom and Dad used at work, and because I
fell in love with video games at age five.

We did have Apple Macintosh IIs in my elementary school classrooms, however, and
I would race to finish my work early so I could play [SimCity][1] or
[KidPix][2].

I was friends with a kid who lived on my parents' street whose family owned a
Mac. I didn't go over to his house often — we used to play outdoors mostly in
those days — but I remember once or twice sitting down to use his family's
*alien* device that didn't require arcane commands to use. I could point and
click through the simple interface. I thought that simplicity meant it wasn't a
"real" computer.

Later, I purchased one of the first-generation iPods. It was a truly magical
device...

For about two months.

Mine bricked itself one day and I remember being so mad about it that, instead
of trying to replace it or have it serviced, I threw it out and swore to never
own another Apple product.

It wouldn't be until university shortly after that, when I began studying
Information Security, that I would fall in love with macOS. It first grabbed my
attention with its amazing design language. Then, I learned it was a UNIX
operating system that could run most of the apps I needed on a daily basis. The
only thing it couldn't do was game, so I remained fascinated from a distance.

Setting aside my iPod grudge, I saved enough money from my day job to
eventually own a 17" MacBook Pro.

It was a magical machine.

I loved the *experience* of this device immediately. There was endless
configuring, poking around the system, and marveling at the sleek [Leopard][3]
interface. I quickly discovered first- and third-party apps that I can't live
without to this day — shout out to [Little Snitch][4] and [Alfred][5]. Time
Machine is the simplest backup software I've ever used, and the hardware of
every Apple product I've owned since that ill-fated iPod has been exemplary.
MacOS was (and is, to a lesser extent) beautiful and just faded into the
background so I could focus on using the device to solve my problem.

Because I spent so much time playing World of Warcraft back then, I kept my
gaming PC around for another 15 years, but over that time I left the PC
ecosystem and embraced Apple's. I've flirted with Linux [here]({{% relref
"2021-05-03-yubikey-mfa-fedora.md" %}}) and [there]({{% relref
"2020-05-26-moving-from-macos-to-linux.md" %}}), but those were academic
excursions: I keep coming back home to Mac.

As a product — not just a collection of technical specs — the Mac is superior to
any other computing platform I've used in my 35 years of computing. I still
enjoy sitting down to use my Mac every day, and I can't put into words why. It's
just become an extension of myself, I suppose. 

Here's to Apple, on their 50th anniversary. Here's to the Crazy Ones with a vision to create a different kind of computer. Here's to another 50 years.
